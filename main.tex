\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background and Motivation}
Public transport in Oldenburg serves both established residents and newcomers. Newcomers reveal initial barriers less visible to long-term residents. This project focuses on their experiences with public transport in Oldenburg and proposes digital measures to improve their user experience.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
This project supports newcomers in Oldenburg. We define "newcomer" as anyone who has lived or stayed in Oldenburg for less than two years. Newcomers face challenges in the following areas:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Finding the right connection
    \item Navigating/Routing
    \item Buying and choosing tickets
    \item Understanding fares and validity
    \item Payments
    \item Knowing which app to use
    \item Language
    \item Real-time information
\end{itemize}

These challenges are documented in literature (\cite{usability},\cite{DZIEKAN2007489},\cite{mindthegap}) and confirmed by the group's personal experience.

\subsection{Objectives}
This project will follow the Interaction Design Cycle visible in \autoref{fig:interaction-design-cycle} as presented in the lecture.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{interaction_design_cycle.png}
    \caption{Interaction Design Cycle as presented in the lecture.}
    \label{fig:interaction-design-cycle}
\end{figure}

For each step, our objectives are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Gather Insights:} Gather real-life information of what issues people have in public transport and identify which of them especially apply to newcomers.
    \item \textbf{Compile Requirements:} Distill the previous findings into concrete requirements describing the system to be implemented later on.
    \item \textbf{Design:} Create an application-prototype mockup that visually fullfills the compiled requirements.
    \item \textbf{Prototype:} Create a prototype the fullfills the requirements and implements the visual design of the design.
    \item \textbf{Evaluate:} Gather and evaluate feedback from real users and their interaction with the prototype.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Gathering Insights}
\subsection{Methodology}
We focused on inquiries and designed an interview sheet that also works as a survey sheet (see \autoref{fig:inteviewsheet}). The sheet was designed for quick interviews at bus stops. It moves from an about-you section to public transport difficulties, asks which apps people use, and includes free-text fields to reduce interviewer effects.

We also extended our literature research on known public transport issues. We used the following methods for gathering insights:

We collected 54 responses, 38 from newcomers. The survey had sampling bias: distributing it among known contacts skewed the sample toward younger German respondents. The free-text fields also required effort to analyze, since some answers were in German and others were unclear.

\subsection{Interpretation}
\subsubsection{Evaluation of Free-Text Responses}
We evaluated free-text responses qualitatively. Responses describing concrete needs or suggestions became direct input for the prototype. Remarks about missing information or confusing ticket options were translated into design requirements. Responses without a new aspect were assigned to existing categories to keep the analysis consistent. This approach preserves individual perspectives while maintaining comparability.

\subsubsection{Visualizing Results}
We developed automated scripts to analyze the survey responses. These scripts cross-referenced various data points, such as the relationship between a user's residence duration and their understanding of the system, as well as age versus usage frequency. 

We generated graphs showing our findings. \autoref{fig:group_problems} illustrates the hurdles newcomers encounter most.

\section{Compiling Requirements}
\subsection{Scope Definition}
The scope is intentionally limited. The project focuses on how newcomers understand routes, tickets, and fares. Due to the timeframe, the prototype prioritizes interaction design and usability over complete coverage. Features that are simplified or conceptual serve to evaluate whether the interface reduces confusion and supports better transport decisions.

Rides outside Oldenburg are not shown. The VBN network restricts available bus and train data, defining the geographic and technical boundaries.

The prototype focuses on route guidance and basic ticket support. Train pricing uses dummy data. Exact prices are not central to the concept. Different ticket types are not handled separately. The system indicates whether bus rides are free based on available ticket information.

Some user preferences are included in the interface but are not yet connected to the routing logic. For example, bicycle and dog options are displayed as part of the user profile and route preferences, but they do not change the resulting route suggestions in the current prototype. This allows the interface concept to reflect relevant user needs while keeping the technical implementation manageable.

The prototype does not implement an independent routing engine. Route suggestions use available API data and simplified assumptions. Taxi and car routing are not implemented. The prototype is a focused interaction and usability prototype, not a replacement for existing mobility platforms.

\section{Designing}
\subsection{Design Evaluation and Requirement Mapping}

We created a low-fidelity Figma prototype. The goal was to design an interface for users unfamiliar with complex public transport tariffs.

Mapping mockups against Functional and Non-Functional Requirements shows which goals are addressed in the design phase and which are delegated to backend implementation.

\subsubsection{Functional Requirements Check}

The current Figma prototypes focus on the ticket optimization flow rather than complete routing mechanics.

\minorheading{Addressed in Design}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR1 (Route Planning for Oldenburg):}  
    The start screen establishes the geographical and contextual scope of Oldenburg, for example by presenting journeys such as \emph{Hauptbahnhof to Stadtmuseum} within \emph{Zone Oldenburg I}.

    \item \textbf{FR5 (Personalized Ticket Overview):}  
    This requirement is fulfilled in the design. An automated verification step (e.g., ``Automatic check running \dots'') with a step-by-step wizard collects traveler information such as age, bicycles, dogs, and travel intent. The system generates a compact ticket recommendation on the final screen.
\end{itemize}

\minorheading{Missing or Delegated to Implementation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FR2 (Route Details):}  
    The current screens do not display detailed routes with stops, transfers, or modes. This functionality must be designed separately.

    \item \textbf{FR4 (Onboarding/Profile):}  
    Although the UI references an ``Existing pass check,'' the complete onboarding or profile creation flow---where users initially provide this information---is not shown in the current mockups.

    \item \textbf{FR3 (HAFAS API Integration):}  
    As the prototype is static, live API integration cannot be visually demonstrated at this stage.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Non-Functional Requirements Check}

The design emphasizes usability and clarity.

\minorheading{Addressed in Design}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NFR1 (Simple and Beginner-Friendly):}  
    The wizard breaks down complex tariffs into simple questions like ``Who is travelling?'' and ``What should we optimise?''. The reassurance text (``We'll only ask what changes the price'') reduces cognitive load for first-time users.

    \item \textbf{NFR2 (Compact Information):}  
    The ticket overview shows price, validity, and zone information in visual cards and compares the optimized recommendation against a standard Single Ticket.

    \item \textbf{NFR3 (Mobile-Friendly):}  
    The prototype uses a mobile viewport with large touch targets and bottom-anchored action buttons, suitable for a PWA.

    \item \textbf{NFR5 (Trustworthy and Transparent):}  
    The design explains ticket recommendations, shows price comparisons, and uses high-contrast colors (blue and white) for readability.
\end{itemize}

\minorheading{Delegated to Implementation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NFR4 (Up-to-date Data):}  
    Ensuring that departure times and ticket data remain current depends on backend systems and external data sources. This requirement cannot be validated within a static Figma prototype.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Limitations of the Mockup}

Static Figma prototypes only simulate system behaviors. Live HAFAS data retrieval and real-time profile logic cannot execute. The ``Automatic check running'' indicator is a placeholder. Dynamic routing will be validated during implementation.


\section{Prototyping}
\subsection{Technologies}
After gathering insights and requirements, we implemented an interactive prototype. The goal was to make the central concept testable: users search a route, see the connection, and receive fare guidance.

The prototype was implemented as a React and Vite web application. Tailwind CSS was used for styling, Leaflet for map display, and the VBN/HAFAS endpoint for public transport data. Profile and session data are stored locally in the browser using IndexedDB. The application state is managed centrally in a React context, which stores the selected route, trip data, profile settings, passengers, search results, and ticket-flow answers.

This stack provided the features to fulfill our requirements and create a PWA.

Code is separated into screen components and domain modules. Screen components handle the UI and interaction. Domain modules contain the application logic. This separation keeps visual code independent of transport-specific calculations. The code remains easier to maintain and extend since interface changes do not affect core logic.

Functionality such as passenger validation, fare handling, taxi fare calculation, ticket optimization, geometry handling, and HAFAS product classification is therefore implemented outside the visual components. This also makes the corresponding logic easier to test independently from the user interface. Some of these modules are covered by automated tests, especially taxi fares, fare calculation, passenger validation, geometry, and HAFAS mode parsing. The tests help ensure that central calculations and classifications remain stable when the prototype is modified.

\subsection{Implemented Features}
The prototype covers the main user journey. Users search a connection, inspect route details, and get a ticket recommendation or taxi fare. The result list shows departure and arrival times, duration, lines, and transfers. The detail screen shows a map, timeline, and boarding information.

A central feature is the user profile. During onboarding, users can define whether they travel as an adult or child, which tickets they already own, which transport modes they prefer, and whether accessibility support is relevant. The profile can be enabled or disabled in the profile menu. When enabled, it filters route results and influences ticket recommendations.

The profile includes ``prefer low-floor vehicles'' and ``notify driver''. These came from survey responses about accessibility and insecurity at stops. They are not available in DB Navigator. In the current prototype, these options are only represented in the interface and do not trigger real functionality.

The prototype also includes taxi support. If taxis are enabled in the profile, a taxi option is shown at the top of the result list. Selecting it skips the public transport ticket questions and directly shows a taxi fare estimate. The estimate uses the local Oldenburg taxi tariff with separate day and night/Sunday rates.

\subsection{Prototype Workflow}
The workflow starts with onboarding. After creating a profile, the user enters a start and destination, selects departure time, and searches for connections. Public transport connections lead to the journey detail screen and ticket flow. The ticket flow asks trip-relevant questions and shows a recommendation. Taxi connections use a shorter path to the fare estimate.

\subsection{Implementation Decisions}
We kept the interface simple. Mode preferences are managed in the profile instead of adding filters to the route planner. This keeps the search screen clean. Details are separated from result cards: cards show essential information, the detail screen provides the full explanation.

For map display, the prototype uses available journey geometry where possible. If detailed geometry is missing, it falls back to simplified or estimated geometry. This supports visual understanding, but avoids implementing a complete independent routing engine.

\subsection{Requirements Checking}
The prototype fulfils the main functional requirements: it provides route planning for Oldenburg and VBN connections, displays journey details, integrates with the HAFAS endpoint, supports a persistent user profile, and personalizes route and ticket information. It addresses non-functional requirements with a compact, mobile-friendly interface.

Requirements are fulfilled at prototype rather than production level. The route planner uses API data but does not replace a full routing engine. Ticket logic is a simplified model sufficient for testing, not a binding fare system. Profile-based personalization covers visible filtering and recommendations, not all real-world effects.

\section{Technical Limitations}

The prototype has technical limitations from available data sources, project scope, and focus on interaction design. Ticket purchasing is not supported. It would require collaboration with official providers and access to payment and fare infrastructure. The prototype provides ticket guidance instead of purchases.

Ticket prices are static and not automatically updated. Prices may become outdated and represent illustrative rather than binding fare data. This is acceptable for evaluating whether users understand ticket recommendations.

Accessibility features are partly conceptual. Notifying bus drivers about wheelchair users is included as an idea but has no technical functionality. The ``prefer low-floor vehicles'' and ``notify driver'' options are design concepts from survey results. They do not affect routing logic since comparable functionality is not available in DB Navigator or the transport data. These features demonstrate a potential improvement, not a fully implemented service.

Routing is partly estimated. The prototype relies on API data instead of an independent routing engine. Route suggestions depend on the external data source quality. This keeps implementation feasible while allowing evaluation of route presentation usability.

\subsection{Evaluation Method}
We conducted a user study using the System Usability Scale (SUS). SUS provides a high-level view of subjective usability through a 10-item Likert scale. It measures effectiveness and efficiency, yielding a single score from 0 to 100, and is easy to administer.

We chose SUS over the User Experience Questionnaire (UEQ). UEQ assesses broader dimensions like attractiveness and stimulation across 26 items, but its scope extends beyond our goals. Since our prototype targets functional barriers rather than emotional responses, SUS offered a more direct measurement of core usability.

We created an anonymous Google Forms survey titled ``Oldenburg Public Transport App - Usability Study'' (see \autoref{fig:googleFormsSheet}). The survey has four sections:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{About you:} This section gathered demographic data, such as age and time lived in Oldenburg, along with public transport usage habits, including frequency, purpose, and currently used apps.
    \item \textbf{Try the app:} Participants were instructed to open the prototype in a separate browser tab and complete three specific scenarios designed to mimic real situations newcomers face.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Task A} involved planning a one-way weekday trip from Oldenburg Hauptbahnhof (Hbf) to Lappan (city centre).
        \item \textbf{Task B} required the user to read the app's ticket suggestion for the previous connection and pick the ticket they would actually buy.
        \item \textbf{Task C} asked users to adjust their profile to simulate using a four-ride ticket (Vierer-Ticket) and verify that the ticket counter decreased correctly.
    \end{itemize}
    After each task, users rated whether they were able to complete it and how easy it was to perform.
    \item \textbf{System Usability Scale (SUS):} Participants rated ten standard SUS statements regarding their confidence, the app's complexity, consistency, and integration of functions on a scale from 1 to 5.
    \item \textbf{Final Thoughts:} The survey concluded with open-ended questions allowing users to describe what worked well and what needed improvement, as well as whether they would use the app in real life for their public transport trips in Oldenburg.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion of Results}

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{sus_histogram.png}
    \caption{SUS Histogram showing the final score distribution over all 49 users that tested the deployed application.}
    \label{fig:sush}
\end{figure}

The SUS results from 49 participants provide quantitative validation. The application achieved a mean SUS score of 70.9 and a median of 72.5 (\autoref{fig:sush}). 

A SUS score of 68 is the industry average. Our mean of 70.9 places usability above average, categorizing the experience as ``Good''. The median of 72.5 shows that more than half of participants rated the system highly. 

Most users scored between 60 and 100. The most frequent scores were in the 80--90 range (14 participants). This concentration indicates that the wizard-based ticket selection and compact route overviews reduced cognitive load and addressed the issues identified during insight gathering.

The results validate our approach and show the prototype assists newcomers despite a small cluster of lower scores (30--50). These likely correspond to edge cases or technical limitations rather than a core concept failure. The 70.9 SUS score confirms the interaction design cycle produced a user-friendly solution for our target group.

\subsection{Conclusion}
This project applied the Interaction Design Cycle to address challenges faced by newcomers in Oldenburg. Surveys and inquiries identified ticket complexity and routing confusion. The prototype prioritized clarity and guided ticket selection over an extensive feature set. The above-average SUS score confirms that the design interventions improved usability. Despite technical limitations, the prototype demonstrates that a user-centric interface can lower the barrier to entry for public transport systems.

\subsection{Future Work}
Future work includes integrating a live ticket purchasing system through collaboration with transport providers. Replacing static pricing with a real-time routing engine would improve accuracy. Accessibility features like the driver notification system should be fully implemented to evaluate real-world impact. Expanding the scope to include regional travel would provide a more comprehensive solution.
